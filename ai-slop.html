Spotting & Surviving AI Slop

What "good enough" becomes when it becomes the whole strategy.

AI slop is low-effort, generic, repetitive content that looks polished at first glance but says very little. It shows up in blog posts, code, product copy, design, and even entire apps. Here's how to recognize it — and how to stop making it.

01 It Sounds Confident, But Empty

Slop loves broad statements: "in today's fast-paced world," "unlock your full potential," "seamlessly leverage synergies." The phrasing is smooth and certain, but if you stop and ask what did that actually say?, the answer is: not much. Confidence is free for a language model — it costs nothing to sound authoritative about nothing.

⚠️ The tell

If you can remove a sentence and the paragraph loses zero meaning, that sentence was slop.

02 It Repeats Itself

The same idea shows up three different ways in three different paragraphs — not because it's building on a point, but because the model is padding for length. You read a wall of text and walk away with one thin thought that could've been a sentence.

"I asked for an intro. I got 600 words that mean 'in conclusion, our company does things, and those things are good.'"

03 It Avoids Specifics

Real writing names tradeoffs, numbers, constraints, and examples. Slop stays safely generic — it never commits to an opinion, a figure, or a real-world case, because specifics are where you can be wrong. Generic claims are un-falsifiable, and un-falsifiable claims are the slop's natural habitat.

🔍 Compare

Slop: "Our platform delivers powerful, scalable solutions for modern teams."
Real: "Our platform runs on 4 regions, auto-scales to 10k req/s, and costs $0.02 per build."

04 It Has No Point of View

Good content makes a call. It takes a side, recommends an approach, warns you off a bad one. Slop tries to please everyone and ends up memorable to no one — a heap of "on the other hand" that never reaches a conclusion. If a piece could have been written about any product in any industry, it wasn't written about yours.

Why AI slop happens

🎯

Asking for a finished result, not a draft

When you want the AI to hand you a done thing, it optimizes for surface polish — which is exactly where slop lives.

⚡

Optimizing for speed and volume

"Generate ten posts by noon" is a slop factory. Quantity without editing is just faster garbage.

🚫

No one edits for voice or accuracy

The raw output ships untouched. No human point of view, no fact-check, no taste applied.

💬

Vague prompts in, vague content out

"Write something about marketing" gives you the median of everything ever written about marketing — which is slop.

How to avoid making it

The antidote to slop is the same as it's always been: an opinion, some evidence, and a human willing to cut. Treat AI output as a rough first draft, not a finished product. Then do the work the model can't.

✅ The fix

Use AI as a draft partner — brainstorm, outline, unblock. Then edit hard for clarity, real examples, and a point of view the reader can't get anywhere else.

Prompting to reduce slop

You can keep slop out before it's written by changing how you ask. Give the model a target voice, a specific audience, real constraints, and a reason to commit to an opinion. The more you nail down, the less room there is for generic filler.

📝 Slop-proof prompt ingredients

Role & voice: "You're a senior engineer who's shipped this."
Specifics: Name the tech, the numbers, the tradeoff.
Constraints: Word count, format, what not to say.
An opinion: "Take a position and defend it — don't hedge."

The bottom line

AI slop isn't a technology problem; it's an editing problem. The models are happy to write well — or badly — depending on what you ask for and what you accept.
